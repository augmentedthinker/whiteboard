The parable

There was once a cartographer who spent years drawing maps of distant kingdoms. The maps were exquisite—careful coastlines, elegant mountains, little compass roses that seemed to promise orientation. But one morning he realized that whenever he stood at a real crossroads, he still hesitated. He knew how to represent direction better than he knew how to choose it.

So he made himself a different instrument. Not a map of everywhere, but a small lantern that only lit the next ten steps. It could not tell him the whole shape of the world. It could only reveal the next stretch of ground, enough to move without pretending to possess the whole horizon. And with that humble light, he began to travel farther than he ever had with all his perfect maps.

Maybe that is what wisdom becomes in a life like ours. Not total clarity. Not a final system. Just enough light to convert thought into motion—then enough honesty to admit when the road has changed.
